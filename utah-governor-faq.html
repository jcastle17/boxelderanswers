Frequently Asked Questions

Stratos Project Area

Overview

What is the project?

The Stratos Project Area is described as an energy and technology infrastructure project in western Box Elder County. It is connected to a large data and energy campus intended to support artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and defense-related operations.

How large is the proposed data center?

The larger project area covers about 40,000 acres. Most of that land is described as remaining undeveloped, with the actual data center footprint using only part of the total area. Remaining land is described as open space that may allow wildlife corridors, continued grazing, and separation from the Great Salt Lake.

What benefits are being promoted?

The listed benefits include private investment, long-term state and local revenue, construction jobs, permanent jobs, and public infrastructure funding.

Process and Governance

What has been approved so far?

MIDA approved creation of the Stratos Project Area on April 24, 2026. Box Elder County later approved an interlocal agreement and resolution connected to the new MIDA project area on May 4, 2026.

Did those votes approve the entire project?

No. The approvals are presented as the beginning of a multi-step process. Future phases are expected to include planning, infrastructure coordination, state permitting, and public engagement.

What studies and approvals are still required?

Several state agencies are expected to review or oversee parts of the project:

  • Air quality review for projected emissions and required controls.
  • Drinking water review for public water systems, source approval, facilities, operators, and reporting.
  • Water quality permits for surface water and groundwater discharges.
  • Water rights review for any refiled water-right change application.
  • Wildlife review and recommendations after formal plans are submitted.

Will there be more public input?

More public comment opportunities are described as part of state review and permitting. A water-right change application includes a 20-day public comment period. Certain air-quality permits may include a 30-day public comment period and a public hearing if requested.

Why is MIDA involved?

The project is described as connected to energy resilience, computing power, data storage, national defense operations, and critical infrastructure.

How can residents and local officials stay involved?

The process is expected to continue over multiple years. A Design Review Committee is also expected, with local representation and public meetings.

Water and Environmental Impact

Do data centers use a lot of water?

The project is described as using newer cooling technology rather than older evaporative cooling methods. The proposed system is described as a closed-loop chilling system with dry, air-based cooling and no continuous water draw.

What is a closed-loop system?

  • Cooling fluid stays inside sealed pipes and equipment.
  • The fluid is not exposed outside the system.
  • It is not consumed during regular operation.
  • It is reused continuously.
  • Water use is described as limited to the initial fill and minor maintenance.

Where would the water come from?

The water is described as coming from private landowners through existing private-property water rights. It is described as not coming from the Great Salt Lake.

Could it affect the Great Salt Lake or groundwater?

The water is described as existing agricultural water rather than new water. Ongoing campus demand is described as closer to a large office complex, with most regular use tied to employee facilities. Water-quality permits would be required for discharges to state waters.

Will it be on the lake’s edge?

The nearest data center is described as about 10 miles from the Great Salt Lake, with open space separating the project from the lake.

What environmental review remains?

The developer is described as needing state approvals and permits, including air quality, water quality, drinking water, and water rights approvals.

Energy and Infrastructure

Will it use the existing power grid?

The project is described as producing all power on site through stand-alone power generation, without adding pressure to the existing grid.

Will it raise Utah power bills?

The stated answer is no, because the campus would be powered independently by a newly built on-site generation plant.

Economic Impact and Community Benefits

What benefits are listed for Box Elder County residents?

The project is described as bringing projected permanent jobs in skilled trades, logistics, IT, and administration, along with construction jobs over a ten-year period. Tax revenues are described as available for county needs, including infrastructure and schools.

What revenue is projected for the county?

Initial phases are described as producing about $30 million in new annual county revenue. At full build-out, projected revenue is described as reaching up to $108 million annually from the energy and data center complex alone. The developer is also described as committing upfront funding to help offset early impacts on services such as law enforcement and fire.

How could agriculture be affected?

Most of the project area is described as seasonal livestock grazing land rather than crop production. The developer has indicated grazing may continue in many areas while infrastructure is phased in, and large portions of the site are expected to remain open.
