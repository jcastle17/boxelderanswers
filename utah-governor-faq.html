Stratos Project Area

Overview

What is the project?

The Stratos Project Area is an energy and technology infrastructure initiative in western Box Elder County. The stated purpose is to strengthen national security and Utah’s long-term economic competitiveness.

The project supports development of a large-scale data and energy campus intended to help power artificial intelligence, cloud computing, and mission-critical defense operations.

How big is the proposed data center?

The full MIDA project area is approximately 40,000 acres. The actual data center footprint is described as only a fraction of that full project area.

The overall acreage is expected to include different types of power generation, and those power sources may have different land footprints. Solar generation would require more land than natural gas generation.

Most of the remaining land is described as staying undeveloped and available as open space, wildlife corridors, continued grazing land, and distance from the Great Salt Lake.

The official FAQ also claims that Salt Lake City is closer to the Great Salt Lake than the proposed data center.

What are the project benefits?

The promoted benefits include significant private investment, long-term local and state revenue, thousands of construction jobs, permanent careers, and funding for needed public infrastructure.

Process and Governance

What has been approved related to creation of the project area?

On April 24, 2026, the Military Installation Development Authority Board of Directors approved creation of the Stratos Project Area in western Box Elder County.

On May 4, 2026, the Box Elder County Commission approved an interlocal agreement and a resolution agreeing to the new MIDA project area.

Did these votes approve the full project?

No. The official FAQ describes those votes as the beginning of a multi-step process, not final approval of the entire project.

Development is expected to happen in phases over multiple years. The remaining process includes additional planning, infrastructure coordination, state regulatory permitting, and community engagement.

MIDA is expected to create a Design Review Committee similar to other MIDA project areas. That committee is described as being largely represented by Box Elder County expert staff and a local landowner. Design Review Committee meetings are public.

What studies and approvals are still required before construction can begin?

Multiple state agencies are expected to provide oversight and review.

  • Division of Air Quality: evaluates projected air pollutant emissions and mandates specific control techniques.
  • Division of Drinking Water: reviews drinking-water systems and ensures drinking water is physically separate from water used for cooling, fire protection, or power generation.
  • Drinking-water oversight: includes source approval, facilities, certified operators, and reporting to support public health and safety.
  • Division of Water Quality: requires permits for surface-water and groundwater discharges to waters of the state, including the Great Salt Lake.
  • Division of Water Rights: evaluates water availability and water-right change applications.
  • Division of Wildlife Resources: may review potential impacts and provide recommendations to minimize or mitigate impacts to wildlife once a formal plan is submitted.

What happened with the water-right change application?

The Division of Water Rights received a water-right change application on March 25. That application was withdrawn on May 6.

The applicant indicated intent to refile and provide more information. Each water-right application follows the same legal process, including a 20-day public comment period.

Will there be more opportunities for public input?

Additional public-comment opportunities are expected as the project moves through state review and permitting.

  • Every water-right change application submitted to the Division of Water Rights includes a 20-day public comment period.
  • No Notice of Intent has been submitted to the Division of Air Quality as of the FAQ.
  • An air-quality draft permit would be subject to a 30-day public comment period and a public hearing if requested.
  • Division of Water Quality permits may involve public notice requirements intended to ensure community awareness and involvement.

Why is MIDA involved?

The Undersecretary of the Air Force asked MIDA to identify locations for independent energy and computing power. The stated reason is that energy resilience, computing power, and data storage are critical for defense operations.

The Stratos Project Area is also described as supporting federal executive orders addressing national energy resilience and development of critical infrastructure.

How can the public, local officials, and county staff stay involved?

Because development is expected to occur in phases over multiple years, the public process is not described as finished. Future steps include planning, infrastructure coordination, state regulatory permitting, and community engagement.

The future Design Review Committee is identified as another way local staff and public meetings remain part of the process.

Water and Environmental Impact

Don’t data centers use a lot of water?

Historically, data centers often relied on evaporative cooling systems and grid-connected power sources, which could indirectly require significant water.

The Stratos project is described as different because it would use a closed-loop chilling system combined with dry, air-based cooling. The FAQ states there is no continuous water draw.

What is a closed-loop system?

The cooling system is described as sealed and reusable.

  • Cooling fluid circulates entirely within sealed piping and equipment.
  • The fluid is never exposed to the outside environment.
  • The fluid is not consumed during operation.
  • The same fluid is reused continuously.

Water is described as being used only for the initial one-time system fill and minor maintenance.

Where will the water come from?

The development team is purchasing water from private landowners. The water is described as not coming from the Great Salt Lake.

The systems are described as using existing water rights attached to private property. The official FAQ claims this creates lower net consumption than current agricultural or ranching use and describes that reduction as a net benefit to the Great Salt Lake watershed.

The FAQ also states that the advanced closed-loop system would create less net consumption compared with traditional data center operations.

Will this project affect the Great Salt Lake or local groundwater?

The project water is described as not coming from the Great Salt Lake and not being new water. It is described as water currently used for agricultural irrigation and connected to water rights of property owners from whom land is being acquired.

Because the cooling systems reuse water internally, ongoing water demand at the campus is projected to be similar to a large office complex. Most regular water use is described as employee-related needs such as restrooms, sinks, and employee facilities.

The Division of Water Quality requires permits for all surface-water and groundwater discharges to waters of the state, including the Great Salt Lake. The FAQ identifies that permitting as the primary mechanism for pollution prevention.

Will the project be on the lake’s edge?

The nearest data center is described as about 10 miles away from the Great Salt Lake, separated by an expansive stretch of open space.

The FAQ again states that Salt Lake City is closer to the Great Salt Lake than the proposed data center.

What air quality, water rights, and environmental reviews are still required?

The developer must comply with state approvals and permits. The FAQ lists significant studies, approvals, and permits still needed, including air quality, water quality, and drinking-water permits from the Department of Environmental Quality, plus water-rights approvals from the Department of Natural Resources.

Energy and Infrastructure

Will the project draw from the existing grid or generate its own power?

The development is described as producing all power on site. It is described as stand-alone power that will not add pressure to the existing grid.

Will this raise power bills for Utah residents?

The FAQ answer is no. It states that a newly constructed on-site power-generation plant will independently power the campus.

Economic Impact and Community Benefits

What benefits would Box Elder County residents receive?

At full build-out, the developer has committed to a projected 2,000 permanent jobs for county residents in skilled trade, logistics, information technology, and administrative positions.

The project is also described as providing thousands of construction jobs over a ten-year period.

Tax revenues may be used at the county’s discretion to support infrastructure and community needs, including local schools. Public infrastructure for the project area is described as being paid for by the developer, not county taxpayers.

During and after construction, land is described as being leased back to local ranchers for grazing.

What benefits would Box Elder County receive?

In the initial phases, the county is expected to receive about $30 million in new annual revenue.

At full build-out, projected annual revenue could increase to $108 million from the energy and data center complex alone. The project is also described as expanding the county’s broader tax base by adding jobs to the local economy.

The developer has committed $16.2 million in upfront funding to offset initial county-budget impacts for essential services such as law enforcement and fire.

How will this impact agriculture in the area?

Most of the project area is described as seasonal livestock grazing land, not crop production. Much of the land is described as difficult to farm and only supporting limited agricultural use today.

The developer has indicated grazing can continue in many areas as infrastructure is phased in. Large portions of the site are expected to remain open even at full buildout.
